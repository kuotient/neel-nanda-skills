**"Verbalizable Representations Form a Global Workspace in Language Models"** — a commentary

This paper introduces the **Jacobian lens** (J-lens) — an averaged-Jacobian readout of the residual stream — and uses it to argue that Claude-family models maintain a privileged, small, broadcast-like subset of internal representations (the **J-space**) that behaves like an access-consciousness "global workspace." I'll take it apart into claims that can be true or false independently, because they very much can, and the paper's own confidence is not distributed the way its title is.

## The claims, decomposed

1. **Scientific (the intermediates claim).** Models compute unspoken intermediate variables mid-forward-pass, store them as identifiable directions in the residual stream, and *use* them causally downstream. (E.g. "spider" appears at intermediate layers on a two-hop prompt and swapping it to "ant" changes the answer 8→6.)
2. **Scientific (the selectivity/privilege claim).** There is a distinguished *subset* of representations — verbalizable, small (≤10% of activation variance), broadcast-composing — that drives deliberate/flexible computation but *not* automatic tasks. This is the claim that turns "there are intermediate variables" into "there is a workspace."
3. **Methodological.** The J-lens is a good, causally-grounded instrument for finding and reading that subset — better than logit lens / tuned lens for surfacing unverbalized intermediates.
4. **Pragmatic.** You can use this for alignment auditing (surfacing hidden goals/deception) and for behavior-shaping (counterfactual reflection training).
5. **Interpretive/philosophical.** This structure is meaningfully "a global workspace" in the access-consciousness sense.

**Lead with what matters.** The title sells claim 5, and the discourse will fixate on claim 5, but the claim that actually *matters* — the one whose truth would reshape how I model a forward pass — is **claim 2, selectivity/privilege**, and its evidential engine is **claim 1**. If claim 1 is real (intermediates stored and used) that's important but not shocking; a lot of prior work already points there and my prior is high (below). What would be genuinely new is claim 2: that the model draws a *functional line* between a small "verbalizable" set that supports flexible, cross-circuit reuse and everything else. That's the load-bearing scientific novelty. So: **claim 2 = most important, and I'm moderately-but-not-fully persuaded.** Claim 1 = important, high confidence. Claim 3 = a means, I'm partially persuaded and it interests me less than the authors need it to. Claim 4 = the weakest evidence in the paper, and I'd discount it. Claim 5 = the one I'm **least qualified** to judge and I'll hold back on it — not out of politeness, but because I think it's doing rhetorical work the empirics don't require.

## Why it should (or shouldn't) work — my prior, before the evidence

My independent model, set down before crediting the paper: if a transformer performs two or three reasoning steps inside a single forward pass, the intermediate results *must* be written somewhere between the steps, and the only channel between layers is the residual stream. So intermediate variables as residual-stream directions is close to forced — I'd bet on it at high odds regardless of this paper. That means the bar for claim 1 is not "does such a thing exist" but "did you catch the *specific* variable and show it's *used*, not just decodable."

Worked example to fix intuition: "legs on the animal that spins webs." The model has to resolve "spider" as an unnamed bridge before it can retrieve "8." If several downstream retrieval circuits (legs? eyes? habitat?) might need "the animal in question," it is *efficient* to hold one consistent "spider" direction any circuit can read — rather than recompute it per circuit. That efficiency argument is exactly why a shared, reusable, broadcast-y direction is plausible, and it's the structure the paper claims to expose. Good: the paper isn't asking me to believe something from nothing; it's supplying evidence for a structure I already had a prior on.

But the same first-principles reasoning makes me *sceptical of the extra step* the paper takes. "There's a reusable direction for the current bridge variable" does **not** entail "there's a privileged workspace." The boring reading of my own efficiency argument is: *whatever variable this circuit needs next is, by construction, the thing that's linearly readable and causally live right now* — and calling that set "the workspace" may just be relabeling "the variables currently in use" with a neuroscience noun. So the selectivity claim (claim 2) is where I most want the paper to beat a boring hypothesis, and it's exactly where it's hardest.

## The evidence, graded — with the boring hypothesis named first each time

**The two-hop intermediate swap (Fig. 13/15) — compelling, and the paper's real spine.** Spider appears at intermediate layers, swap to ant flips 8→6; top-1 flip on 54/70/70% of trials across Haiku/Sonnet/Opus; and — the part I care about — intermediate swaps take effect **~17% earlier in depth** than answer swaps. Boring hypothesis: the lens is just reading a smeared blend of input and output, and "spider" is decodable but epiphenomenal — a correlational lens skipping ahead. What kills the boring version here is the *causal* swap changing the answer in the semantically-correct direction (8→6, not to noise) **and** the depth-ordering (intermediate before answer). A pure readout artifact has no reason to produce a coherent counterfactual answer or a clean temporal precedence. This is the closest thing to hard-to-fake evidence in the paper. It's not fully decisive — 54–70% is a real miss rate, and I'd want the failure trials characterized — but it does update me on claim 1.

**The J-space vs non-J-space decomposition (Fig. 8, Fig. 16) — this is the cruxy result for claim 2, and it's compelling.** They split a concept vector into its J-space and non-J-space components and show only the J-space part drives verbal report (59% vs 5%) and intermediate reasoning (61% vs 28%; clamping J-space drops the non-J-space effect to 6%). This is the single most important experiment in the paper, because it's the one that tries to establish that the *subset* is privileged rather than that *some* direction works. Boring hypothesis: the J-space component is simply the part of the vector with larger projection onto directions the unembedding/downstream can read — i.e. "the component that does something is the component that does something," a near-tautology given how J-space is *defined* (via the Jacobian to the output). The paper doesn't fully escape this — the decomposition is defined by the same operator whose privilege it's demonstrating, so there's circularity risk. What partially rescues it is that the 5% / 6% floors are genuinely low, and that the *same* split predicts across two different task families (report and reasoning). I read this as compelling-but-not-decisive: the privilege is real relative to *this* decomposition; whether "verbalizable" is a natural kind or an artifact of choosing the output-Jacobian basis is not settled.

**Selectivity: ablate the workspace, automatic tasks survive (Fig. 20, 21, 24) — compelling, and my favorite result for its *asymmetry*.** Spanish→French swap flips explicit language report and downstream inference (García Márquez→Victor Hugo) but leaves Spanish *continuation* and anomaly detection untouched; top-10 J-lens ablation craters multi-hop reasoning to ~zero while MMLU/SQuAD/sentiment stay near baseline; line-counting appears in the lens only when the task needs it (20 positions vs 0). Boring hypothesis: ablating any high-variance directions breaks hard compositional tasks and spares easy classification ones, because hard tasks are just more fragile — nothing "workspace"-specific. What makes this better than that: the *double dissociation* — the swap changes the deliberate output to a coherent alternative (French/Hugo/Bonjour) while automatic Spanish processing continues *correctly*. Fragility-of-hard-tasks predicts degradation, not a clean steer-to-a-specific-wrong-answer on one route with the other route intact. That asymmetry is the strongest single piece of support for claim 2. I'd still want a matched control that ablates 10 *random high-norm* directions and shows it *doesn't* produce the same dissociation; the paper mentions matched-norm/random controls in methods but I couldn't verify they were run on this specific ablation.

**Directed modulation / "white bear" (Fig. 9–11) — suggestive, not load-bearing.** "Concentrate on citrus" → orange in the lens; "ignore X" surfaces X *more* (white-bear effect); part-of-speech only surfaces "adjective" when the question demands it. Cute and consistent, but easily confounded: instruction-following models put instruction-relevant tokens high in *many* internal readouts, and the white-bear parallel is a nice story rather than evidence of workspace structure specifically. I wouldn't hang anything on it.

**Flexible generalization (Fig. 18–19) — mildly interesting, honestly reported.** France→China swap generalizes across capital/language/continent templates: but only 76/192 succeed at basic strength, 101/192 at double. That's below half at the honest setting, and success correlates with "workspace loading." The correlation cuts both ways — it's consistent with a real reuse story, but also with the mundane reading that swaps work when the direction was strongly present and fail otherwise (i.e. the method works when it works). To their credit the authors say exactly this. I read it as weak positive evidence with a built-in escape hatch.

**Structural signatures — kurtosis / autocorrelation / next-token accuracy all localizing to L38–92 (Fig. 28) — suggestive and pretty, but I lean on it least among the "science."** Three metrics agreeing on the same middle-third transition is aesthetically compelling and I buy that *something* changes regime in the middle layers. Boring hypothesis: middle layers of deep transformers are where abstract/semantic processing lives and early/late layers are token-local — a well-worn observation. Three metrics agreeing that "the middle is the abstract part" doesn't specifically evidence a *workspace*; it re-derives a known layered structure and gives it a workspace name. Consistent with the thesis, not diagnostic of it.

**"Ignition" (Fig. 29) — mildly interesting, the most theory-laden reading.** 50/50 concept-mixture inputs tracked smoothly early, then snap to one endpoint at a threshold from ~L38. The sharp nonlinearity is real and neat. But naming it "ignition" imports a specific neuroscientific commitment; the boring version is that a downstream layer with a decision-like nonlinearity (softmax-ish competition, winner-take-all) produces thresholding — which any categorization network does. I'd call the phenomenon real and the label speculative.

**Broadcast/composition ("mechanistically privileged," compose more broadly upstream *and* downstream) — I can't grade this, because I couldn't find the number.** This is frustrating, because it's the structural claim that would most directly earn the word "workspace" (broadcast is the defining property of a global workspace). The prose asserts J-lens vectors compose more broadly than other vectors, but I was unable to retrieve the metric or the quantitative separation from the sections I read. If that result is as clean as the causal-swap results, it's potentially the second cruxy pillar; as it stands I have to mark it *unassessed*, and a reader should not take the broadcast claim as demonstrated on the strength of what I saw.

**Applications — the weakest evidence in the paper; I'd discount all three for now.** Alignment auditing (surfacing "reward"/"bias"/"secretly"/"trick" in misaligned models) is qualitative, cherry-picked cases, no false-positive/false-negative rates, no baseline. It's a *demo*, and the boring hypothesis — that on a model literally trained to be sycophantic you can find "reward" high in *lots* of readouts, including logit lens — is not excluded. The assistant-perspective section is explicitly anecdotal. Counterfactual reflection training is the most interesting of the three because it has a causal loop (train to reflect → concepts appear in J-space → ablate → behavior reverts), but the magnitude, test set, and generalization aren't reported in what I read. I'd rate reflection-training "cool, worth a real study," and the auditing application "not yet evidence."

## Methodological read on the J-lens itself

The J-lens is a genuinely nice idea: replacing "apply unembedding to layer ℓ" (logit lens, J=I) with "apply the *averaged Jacobian* from ℓ to output, then unembed" gives a first-order-correct linearization instead of an uncorrected one, and the argument that tuned-lens's correlational objective "skips ahead" to the output while a causal Jacobian surfaces intermediates is principled and matches the two-hop results. That's a real methodological contribution independent of the workspace framing.

Two reservations keep me from full confidence in claim 3. **(a) Faithfulness is established causally, not against ground truth.** The paper never validates the lens on a setting with a *known* intermediate; it argues faithfulness from the fact that interventions behave sensibly. That's a decent argument but it's the same operator grading its own homework — the swaps are defined in the lens's own coordinate system. **(b) The single-token vocabulary restriction is a real limit**, honestly flagged: the lens only sees concepts aligned with single tokens, so "the J-space" is partly "the part of the model's cognition that happens to be one token away from the vocabulary." How much of the claimed selectivity is a fact about the model versus a fact about which concepts the instrument can see is not resolved, and it should temper how literally one reads "small subset of total representation."

## Practical read — bounded

For **hypothesis generation in interpretability and auditing**: I'd reach for the J-lens early, exactly as you'd use logit lens today but expecting it to surface unspoken intermediates the logit lens misses. Treat a lens hit as a lead worth a causal follow-up, not a finding.

Where it **fails**: multi-token concepts, anything mis-aligned to the token basis, and — critically for the auditing pitch — validation. Do *not* deploy "the workspace showed 'deceive', therefore the model is deceptive" as a detector. The applications section has no false-positive discipline, and a readout that fires on relevant tokens will fire on a *lot* of relevant tokens. Expect false positives on adversarial or ambiguous inputs and expect silence on any deception encoded across multiple tokens or off the vocabulary axis.

What to **realistically expect**: a better lens (I believe this), strong evidence that unspoken intermediates are stored and used (I believe this), moderate evidence that a definable privileged subset supports flexible computation (I lean yes, with the circularity caveat), and a workspace *analogy* that is evocative and partially earned but out ahead of its measurements — especially the broadcast claim I couldn't verify.

## Where I hold back

On claim 5 — whether this is genuinely a "global workspace" in the access-consciousness sense — I decline to render a strong verdict, and I want that decline to read as calibration rather than evasion, given how specifically I've committed above. Two reasons. First, standing: the mapping onto Baars/Dehaene global-workspace theory is a cognitive-science judgment I'm not the right person to adjudicate, and the authors themselves concede the architectural disanalogies (no separable input processors, feedforward-depth substituting for recurrence, no recurrent broadcast). Second, and more within my lane: I think the workspace framing is *severable* from the paper's real contributions. Strip the neuroscience and you still have (a) a principled causal lens and (b) compelling evidence for stored-and-used intermediates plus a nontrivial selectivity dissociation. Those stand on their own. The workspace analogy is the part I'd treat as the least load-bearing — an interpretive overlay that will drive citations and controversy but that the empirics neither require nor fully establish. The authors are commendably careful to disclaim phenomenal consciousness; I'd extend that caution one notch further and treat even the *access*-consciousness label as a hypothesis the paper motivates rather than demonstrates.

Net: the causal two-hop swap and the J-space/non-J-space dissociation are the results carrying this paper; the selectivity double-dissociation is the most striking; the structural signatures and modulation results are texture; the applications are demos; and the workspace framing is a bet, not a finding.
