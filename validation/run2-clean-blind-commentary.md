## Commentary: "Verbalizable Representations Form a Global Workspace in Language Models"

### The claims, decomposed

This paper is doing at least four functionally independent things, and they succeed or fail on very different evidence. Separating them is most of the work, because the paper's own framing bundles them into one grand narrative ("LLMs have a global workspace") and the bundling flatters the weakest parts by association with the strongest.

1. **Methodological claim (the J-lens).** The averaged Jacobian `J_ℓ = 𝔼[∂h_final/∂h_ℓ]`, composed with the unembedding, yields per-token "lens vectors" that recover interpretable, *causally load-bearing* concept directions at middle layers where the logit lens reads noise. This is the machinery everything else stands on.

2. **Empirical claim (unspoken intermediates are represented and causal).** For two-hop prompts ("legs on the animal that spins webs"), the intermediate concept (spider) is present in the residual stream at the relevant position, and *swapping its lens vector for another (ant) changes the final answer* (8→6), at a depth *earlier* than swapping the answer itself.

3. **Empirical/structural claim (selectivity — a genuine dissociation).** A small, low-variance subspace (the "J-space," ~10–25 active directions, never >10% of activation variance) is *necessary for flexible inference but not for automatic processing*. The sharp version: the same J-lens swap flips "name the language" and "name an author in that language" on nearly every trial, but has *no effect* on continuation or anomaly-detection on the identical tokens; and ablating the top-k J-lens vectors tanks multi-hop/analogy/translation while leaving MMLU, SQuAD, sentiment "essentially unaffected."

4. **Interpretive/framing claim (this is a global workspace / conscious-access analog).** The five properties (verbal report, directed modulation, internal reasoning, flexible generalization, selectivity) jointly instantiate the functional profile that Global Workspace Theory associates with conscious *access* (explicitly not phenomenal consciousness).

**Which one matters most, and where I land on each.** The claim that *matters most* is not the workspace framing (4) — it's the **selectivity dissociation (3)**, because "there is a small, identifiable, low-variance subspace that is causally required for flexible/verbalizable computation and causally irrelevant to automatic computation" is a real, structural fact about how these models are organized, and if it holds it changes how you'd audit, steer, and reason about a model regardless of whether you ever say the word "workspace." It is also, conveniently, the claim best supported by hard-to-fake evidence. Claim (2) I find compelling and it's the load-bearing micro-result under (3). Claim (1) I'm fairly persuaded is a *real improvement* over the logit lens but I think it's oversold as a new kind of object. Claim (4) is the most important *if true in the strong sense* but it's the one I'm least persuaded by and partly outside my standing — and crucially, nothing in claims 1–3 actually requires it. I'll commit hard on 2 and 3, moderately on 1, and hold back on 4.

### Why it should (or shouldn't) work — my prior, before the evidence

My independent model, before weighing their results: a transformer computes a next-token distribution, so *by construction* there is a privileged readout direction at the end (the unembedding), and information "flows toward" it as depth increases. The logit lens works precisely because late layers already live in output coordinates. So the *boring* prior is: "verbalizable" = "close to the output in the sense the unembedding measures," and any method that finds such directions is finding a late-stage summary, not a distinct cognitive object.

But there's a second thing my prior says, and it cuts the other way. Multi-hop tasks are the natural stress test. Take "legs on the animal that spins webs → 8." The model *cannot* solve this by output-coordinate readout at the point where "spider" is needed, because "spider" is never emitted — the token that gets emitted is "8." So if a concept ("spider") is (a) recoverable at an intermediate layer, (b) *not* recoverable by a pure output-coordinate lens there, and (c) causally pivotal such that overwriting it changes the arithmetic answer to be *correct for the substituted animal*, then you have separated "represented-and-used-internally" from "about-to-be-said." That separation is the whole game. Under my model, I'd expect exactly this to be findable for two-hop factual/arithmetic chains, and I'd expect it to *degrade* for concepts that don't tokenize cleanly (small integers, multi-token entities). So the interesting question isn't "is there internal structure" — of course there is — it's "did they show the *same* direction is both the internal argument and the verbalizable readout, causally, and not just a correlational shadow of the answer."

That sets my bar. Everything below is graded against it.

### The evidence, graded

**The two-hop intermediate swap (claim 2) — compelling, and this is the cruxy micro-result.** The setup is well chosen and the numbers are non-trivial: the coordinate swap moves the target-appropriate answer to the top on 54% (Haiku) / 70% (Sonnet) / 70% (Opus) of trials. The *boring hypothesis* I'd reach for first: the "spider" lens vector isn't representing spider-as-intermediate at all — it already smuggles in "8," so swapping to "ant" is really just swapping in "6," and this is arithmetic-injection dressed up as concept-injection. What rules it out is the single best control in the paper: the intermediate swap takes effect **~17% earlier in depth** than the answer swap. If the intermediate vector were acting through a smuggled answer component, both would bite at the same depth. This is the kind of result the boring hypothesis genuinely can't survive, and it's why I read (2) as compelling rather than suggestive. The J-space decomposition sharpens it further: swapping only the J-space component of the intermediate probe flips 61% of answers (matching 60% for the raw vector), the non-J-space component alone gets 28%, and with J-space clamped it collapses to 6%. That "6% with J-space removed" is doing real work — it says the effect lives *in the low-variance subspace specifically*, not in bulk activation.

I'd still flag: 54–70% is a solid effect but not overwhelming, and the failure cases (small integers) are exactly where the authors admit the lens may not align. So the mechanism is *real and demonstrated on a subset*, not universal. Honest, and they say so.

**The selectivity dissociation (claim 3) — this is the most hard-to-fake evidence in the paper, and the double-dissociation design is why.** The language-passage experiment is the strongest single thing here: the concept ("Spanish") appears in the J-lens at *comparable rates* across all four tasks (so it's represented everywhere), but the causal swap flips "name the author" (García Márquez→Hugo) and "name the language" on essentially every trial while leaving continuation (still fluent Spanish) and anomaly-detection (intrusion still caught) *completely unmoved*. That is a genuine dissociation: same representation present, causal role present in one task class and absent in the other, on identical tokens. The boring hypothesis — "the swap just isn't strong enough to affect anything" — is killed by the fact that it *does* affect the flexible tasks at near-100%. The other boring hypothesis — "continuation doesn't use language identity at all" — is the interesting subtlety: the model demonstrably *is* producing Spanish, so it "knows" the language, it just doesn't route that knowledge *through the verbalizable subspace*. That's the actual content of "selectivity," and the experiment earns it.

The ablation battery (Figure 24) corroborates at coarser grain: ablating top-k J-lens vectors over L38–L54 selectively guts multi-hop/analogy/translation while MMLU and SQuAD sit at baseline. The boring hypothesis here is the standard ablation trap — "ablating any capable directions degrades hard tasks more than easy ones because hard tasks have less margin." I don't think the ablation *alone* fully escapes this; MMLU-robustness could partly be a difficulty-margin artifact. But the ablation isn't load-bearing on its own — it's redundant confirmation of the *swap* dissociations, which are clean because they're within-token and directional. The chain-of-thought result (math with CoT is far more ablation-robust than the same problems answered directly) is a nice independent triangulation: it's exactly what you'd predict if the J-space is where silent intermediates live, since CoT externalizes those intermediates onto tokens and out of the subspace. I find that mildly-to-moderately convincing — it's consistent, and it's the kind of prediction that could have failed.

**The verbal-report / introspection results (part of claim 1 and 4) — compelling on causality, but here's where the framing outruns the evidence.** The variance decomposition is genuinely striking and I want to credit it: the J-space component carries a *median 6–7% of the concept vector's variance*, yet swapping *that* component alone drives the target into top-5 on 59% of trials (vs. 88% for the full lens vector), while swapping the *other 93%* succeeds on only 5%. So reportability is concentrated in a tiny, low-variance slice — that's a real and non-obvious finding, and it's the empirical backbone of "small privileged subspace." The introspection tie-in (inject a lens vector, model reports it, median reciprocal rank rises dose-dependently with steering strength over n=100) is a nice consistency check.

But now the boring hypothesis for the *whole verbal-report story*: is "J-space" anything more than "the logit lens, computed more carefully"? The authors are refreshingly candid that "the logit lens... captures much of the workspace-like structure identified by the J-lens, though with somewhat lower reliability," and that the two lenses "agree closely in the model's last several layers." This is the load-bearing honesty in the paper. My read: the J-lens is a *real methodological improvement* — the Jacobian correctly handles the fact that mid-layer representations aren't yet in output coordinates, and the two-hop "17% earlier" result is something the logit lens provably could not produce (it "skips ahead to the output"). So (1) clears the bar of "not just a rebrand." But it does *not* clear the bar of "a fundamentally new object" — a lot of the workspace structure is visible to the humble logit lens too, which means the discovery is more "we now have a sharper mid-layer readout" than "we found a hidden module."

**The flexible-generalization result — compelling with an important honest caveat.** Swapping France→China across templates produces China-appropriate cities/languages/continents on 76/192 trials (101/192 at doubled strength). The success rate is frankly mediocre in absolute terms, and I'd normally grade a 40–53% swap as suggestive. What rescues it into "compelling" is the correlation they report: success tracks "workspace loading" (how present the concept already is in J-space on the clean pass). That's a real prediction — it says the effect isn't about the *existence* of a lens vector but the *representational status* of the concept — and it's the kind of internal-consistency signal a confound wouldn't naturally produce. Still, sub-60% ceilings across these swaps are a reminder that the "workspace" is leaky, not a clean bottleneck.

**The applications (auditing, model comparison, counterfactual reflection) — suggestive, proof-of-concept, not load-bearing.** The alignment-auditing readouts ("fake," "fictional," "reward," "trick" surfacing in misaligned models; ablating eval-awareness surfacing concealed malice) are genuinely arresting *demos*, but they're observational and cherry-pickable — no systematic evaluation of whether ablation reliably improves safety outcomes, no base rates, no false-positive analysis. The counterfactual-reflection result is the strongest of the three *conceptually*, because it closes a loop: after training, ethical concepts populate the J-space AND *ablating those implanted representations reverts the behavioral improvement*. That's a nice identity-corroboration (same reps that are verbalized also govern silent behavior). But there's no effect-size quantification across domains, so I file it as suggestive-plus. These sections sell the *vision*; they don't yet certify it.

### The framing claim (4), and where I hold back

The consciousness/global-workspace framing is the claim I'm least qualified to adjudicate and least persuaded of in its strong form, so I'll be explicit about the boundary rather than fake a verdict.

Two separate things are worth keeping apart. First, as a *neutral naming device*: the five properties are real, they're demonstrated (report and modulation and generalization and selectivity all have causal evidence above), and "global workspace" is a reasonable *label* for "a small low-variance subspace that is broadcast to many downstream computations and is the locus of verbalizable/flexible processing." At that level I have no objection — it's a useful compression. Second, as a *substantive analogy to conscious access*: here I mostly decline. The authors themselves disclaim the strong version cleanly ("access consciousness is a purely functional notion... we take no position" on phenomenal consciousness; "we do not claim that language models reproduce the full architecture... no obviously separable input processors... single feedforward pass rather than recurrent loops"). Given those disclaimers, the honest reading is that they've shown a *functional homology on five criteria*, not that they've found consciousness — and the architectural disanalogies they list (no encapsulated parallel processors, no recurrence, everything in one forward pass) are, to my mind, large enough that the mapping to GWT is more evocative than mechanistic. The boring hypothesis for the *framing* is that any sufficiently expressive model with a privileged output pathway will exhibit a "small subspace that's close to report and used for flexible tasks," and calling that a global workspace imports connotations the evidence doesn't license. I don't think the paper *asserts* the connotations — but the title and the five-criteria scaffolding invite the reader to. On the deeper question of whether the GWT analogy is scientifically apt, I have no standing to rule, and I'd rather say so than launder my enthusiasm for the selectivity result into a philosophical position.

One methodological caution I *do* have standing on: the five criteria are somewhat curated to match GWT, and there's mild circularity risk in that the J-lens is *defined* by proximity-to-verbalization and then the workspace is *characterized* by verbalizability. The two-hop and selectivity experiments are what break this circle (they show verbalizability dissociating from mere representation, causally), which is exactly why those are the cruxy results and the framing is not.

### Practical read

**For what:** Use the J-lens/J-space when you have *a specific model behavior you want to account for at the level of "what concept is it silently using here"* — multi-hop reasoning, latent language/topic, or auditing whether an eval-awareness or deception concept is active but unspoken. The within-token directional swap is the reliable instrument; the "17% earlier" logic gives you a real handle on ordering intermediate vs. final computation. Reach for it especially where the logit lens goes dark (mid layers, unverbalized intermediates).

**Where it fails:** (a) Concepts that don't map to single tokens — the lens is vocabulary-bound, and the authors flag small-integer and multi-token failures explicitly; treat a *negative* J-lens reading as "not found by this tool," never as "not represented." (b) The swaps top out at 50–70% even on their best cases, so it's an instrument for *finding and testing candidate representations*, not for exhaustively mapping or *certifying absence* of one. (c) The ablation-based capability claims partly ride on difficulty-margin confounds; don't lean on the ablation battery as independent proof of selectivity — lean on the double-dissociation swaps.

**What to expect:** A sharper mid-layer readout that recovers causal intermediates the logit lens misses, plus a robust demonstration that flexible computation routes through a small privileged subspace while automatic computation doesn't. Do *not* expect a clean bottleneck (it's leaky, ~10–25 fuzzy directions, sub-60% swap ceilings), and do not expect the auditing applications to be turnkey — they're compelling demos awaiting systematic validation. And expect the "global workspace / conscious access" story to be a suggestive frame around solid mechanistic findings, not a settled claim you should repeat unhedged.

**Bottom line:** The load-bearing contributions — the two-hop intermediate-swap with its depth-timing control, and the selectivity double-dissociation — are compelling-to-hard-to-fake and would survive most of the boring hypotheses I can construct. The J-lens is a real improvement but shares more with the logit lens than the framing suggests. The workspace/consciousness framing is a defensible label doing more rhetorical than empirical work, and the authors are (to their credit) careful to disclaim its strong reading. I'd cite this for the selectivity result first, the intermediate-representation result second, and treat the rest as promising scaffolding.
